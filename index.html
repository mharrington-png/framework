An Evaluative Framework
for AI Use in Education

Not all AI applications in education carry the same risks or offer the same value. This evaluative framework profiles each use type across six dimensions — mapping what each deployment demands of teachers, what student capacity it builds or erodes over time, and how deeply the AI enters the learning experience.

Application types
Teacher demand · upper half
What this deployment requires of the professional making and managing it — upstream before use and actively during it.
Student capacity trajectory · horizontal axis
What sustained use builds or erodes in students over time — AI and tool fluency on the left, disciplinary knowledge on the right. A low score on either pole is a warning.
Student reach · lower half
How far the AI reaches into the student's learning experience, and what it demands of students once it is there.

Dimension definitions

Teacher demand Deployment judgment Expertise required to decide whether and how to deploy AI for a task — exercised upstream, before use begins.
Teacher demand Teacher in-use judgment Active professional discernment the teacher must exercise while the tool is running.
Student capacity trajectory Tool fluency Whether sustained use builds transferable AI literacy in students — understanding how these systems behave, where they fail, and how to work with them critically.
Student reach Proximity to learning How directly the AI touches the core acts of instruction, feedback, and assessment.
Student reach Student in-use judgment Critical thinking students must supply to use the tool productively and safely.
Student capacity trajectory Disciplinary knowledge Whether sustained use builds students' content understanding and disciplinary thinking — the knowledge that belongs to them regardless of what tools they use.
Reading the profile: The upper half encodes teacher demand — high scores mean significant professional expertise is required. The lower half encodes student reach — how far the AI extends into learning and what it asks of students. The horizontal axis encodes student capacity trajectory — a low score on either pole is a warning that sustained use is substituting for student growth rather than enabling it. The goal is not a high total: it is an honest profile, because you can only govern what you can see.
Each petal = one dimension  ·  Each arc ring = one point on a 1–5 scale  ·  Filled arcs = scored level  ·  Upper half: teacher demand  ·  Horizontal axis: student capacity trajectory  ·  Lower half: student reach
