An Evaluative Framework for
AI Deployment Decisions

Middlesex School  ·  Concord, Massachusetts

School AI policies establish that appropriate AI use is discipline-specific and determined by departments and teachers, but they offer no structure for how teachers make those determinations. This framework provides structure to those conversations.

Teachers should hold three considerations in mind when evaluating any AI use — their own or their students'.

T
Teacher
What does this tool use do for the teacher — and what does it ask of them?
Deployment judgment Expertise required to decide whether and how to use AI for a task — exercised before use begins.
Teacher in-use agency The professional authority the teacher retains while the tool runs — whether their judgment is still shaping what students experience, or whether the system has taken over the meaningful decisions.
L
Learner
What does this AI use do for the student — and can they reasonably exercise the judgment it requires?
Proximity to learning How directly the AI touches the core acts of instruction, learning, feedback, and assessment.
Student judgment burden The degree of critical judgment students must independently supply for this AI use to go well — not as a condition of access, but as a requirement for the interaction to be educationally sound.
C
Capacities
What capacities does this AI use build in students?
Tool fluency Whether sustained use builds AI literacy — understanding how these systems behave and how to work with them critically.
Disciplinary knowledge Whether sustained use builds students' content understanding and disciplinary thinking.

Shape, not score

Together, the dimensions described above can help profile a particular AI use. They can be mapped to the visual profile here — select a consideration to see which dimensions it corresponds to.

A profile full in the upper half and thin in the lower means significant teacher judgment is required and the AI's reach into student experience is limited — the professional is doing the work. Heavy in the lower half with a thin upper means the AI touches instruction, feedback, or assessment directly, while judgment lands on the student rather than the teacher.

The equator tracks student capacities over time. Both horizontal dimensions trace what sustained use builds or erodes in students. Whether high or low serves your students depends on your curricular goals.

The aim is not to encourage AI uses with high totals. It is to surface what a specific use actually asks of teachers and students — so that judgment can be exercised deliberately.

The profiles below are one teacher's reasoning about specific AI uses he has tried or evaluated at Middlesex School. They are not verdicts — they illustrate how these considerations structure thinking about a concrete scenario. Your context may look different, and that difference is the point.
